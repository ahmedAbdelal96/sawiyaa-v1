# Business Model

Fayed is a two-sided healthcare marketplace with an operations layer.

It connects:

- people who need care
- practitioners who provide care
- administrators and support teams who keep the platform reliable

The business is not just "appointments". The real product is a managed care journey that reduces friction before, during, and after a session.

## Why the platform matters

Healthcare booking is stressful when the journey is fragmented.
Fayed adds value by combining:

- discovery
- matching
- booking
- payment
- session access
- chat
- follow-up
- wallet and refunds
- support and moderation

That combination makes the platform more useful than a simple directory or a basic scheduling tool.

## What the platform actually sells

The platform is built around care access and care operations:

- **Session booking** - the core commercial event.
- **Session management** - the operational layer that keeps bookings reliable.
- **Payment handling** - secure checkout and money flows.
- **Refund handling** - policy-based cancellation and refunds.
- **Practitioner visibility** - a structured marketplace for providers.
- **Training and content** - additional value surface around care and education.
- **Support and moderation** - trust and service quality.

## Marketplace model

Fayed behaves like a managed marketplace:

- the patient side creates demand
- the practitioner side supplies available expertise
- the platform handles discovery, policy, payment, and support

The goal is not to maximize raw volume at any cost.
The goal is to create a trustworthy system where bookings convert, sessions happen, and follow-up is manageable.

## Revenue logic

The codebase and product structure show a business model centered on:

- session-based transactions
- platform-controlled money flow
- wallet and settlement handling
- refund and cancellation policy enforcement
- practitioner payout or settlement operations
- optional training / content expansion paths

This docs set should not invent a fee model that the code does not currently enforce. If the implementation changes, update this document to match the contract.

## Business outcomes that matter

The platform succeeds when it can:

- convert visitors into booked sessions
- keep practitioners active and available
- keep payment and refund flows understandable
- maintain trust in chat, support, and moderation
- reduce operational overhead through clearer self-service flows
- support growth into more specialties and services without breaking the core model

## Product moat

The platform's strength comes from the combination of:

- healthcare-specific trust
- structured session workflows
- bilingual user experience
- policy-driven money handling
- operational support and moderation
- a calm design language that reduces decision friction
