# Strategy and Vision

This document explains where Fayed came from, what the product is trying to become, and which ideas were carried forward from the platform’s original foundation docs.

## The original platform thesis

Fayed began as a healthcare platform for connecting people who need care with practitioners who can provide it.

The early foundation documents describe a platform that is more than a booking tool:

- it reduces confusion before booking
- it guides users toward the right practitioner or next step
- it supports sessions, payment, and follow-up
- it keeps the experience calm and trustworthy

That thesis still holds.

## The current strategic direction

The platform now has two layers:

### 1. Platform Core

The core handles the operational backbone:

- authentication and identity
- practitioners and patients
- sessions and scheduling
- availability and presence
- payments, refunds, wallet, and settlements
- support and moderation
- content and training
- admin operations and diagnostics

### 2. Care Experience Layer

The newer layer turns the platform into a guided journey instead of a static listing:

- guided entry points
- guided matching
- assessments and self-discovery
- session booking hardening
- support as a product surface
- therapy journey / patient journey views
- continuity and follow-up signals

This is the part that makes the product feel meaningfully different from a simple directory.

## What problem the product solves

Fayed reduces the friction people face when trying to start care:

- not knowing which practitioner to choose
- not knowing what kind of help they need
- not knowing whether a session is available now
- not knowing what happens after payment
- not knowing how cancellation and refunds work
- not knowing where to go when they need support

The product’s job is to make the next step feel obvious.

## Lessons carried from the Shezlong reference

The Shezlong case study and product reference were useful because they show how a healthcare marketplace can combine trust, guided discovery, and operational clarity.

### What we take

- guided matching above basic filtering
- assessments as part of the funnel, not just side content
- support as a live product surface
- a structured patient journey
- task-specific booking and payment flows
- trust signals around privacy, moderation, and practitioner credibility

### What we do not copy

- crowded layouts
- generic SaaS visuals
- old or confusing terminology
- listing-only thinking without decision support
- unnecessary complexity in the interface
- copy or branding that does not belong to Fayed

## Business implication

This strategy changes how the platform should grow:

- the core must stay stable
- the care experience layer should increase conversion and trust
- support should reduce abandonment and recovery costs
- assessments should improve matching quality
- journey views should improve continuity and retention

## Product priorities implied by the foundation docs

The original documents point to a clear order of importance:

1. Keep the booking, payment, and session core reliable.
2. Add guided matching so users feel helped, not lost.
3. Add assessments to support discovery and confidence.
4. Make support part of the product experience.
5. Present the patient area as a journey, not a pile of unrelated pages.
6. Add continuity and follow-up signals once the core is stable.

## What this means for documentation

When future docs describe the platform, they should not only list pages and routes.
They should explain:

- why the product exists
- what user problem it solves
- how the marketplace works
- how the platform makes money
- how the operating team keeps it safe
- how guided care changes the patient journey
